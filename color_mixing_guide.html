Step 01

Secondary Colors

Mix two primary colors in equal parts. The ratio is the secret — shift proportions to travel between warm and cool variations of the same color family.

The Primary Triangle

Red, blue, and yellow sit opposite each other. Mixing any two creates a secondary — orange, green, or violet. These three secondaries form a second triangle inside the wheel.

The ratio is everything: 2 parts red + 1 part blue makes a warm burgundy-purple. Flip those proportions for a cool violet.

Step 04

White & Black

Two powerful modifiers that can transform any color into something completely different. Use them with intention — a little goes a long way.

+ White

Adds airiness and softness. Pushes any color toward its pastel version. White dominates quickly — always add it to the color, not the other way around.

✦ Pro tip: Start with your color, then add white one tiny touch at a time. You can always add more but you can't take it back.

+ Black

Deepens and darkens. Too much will "dirty" the color and lose its vibrancy. Use sparingly — it's far more powerful than it looks on the tube.

✦ Pro tip: Try a dark complement instead of black — deep blue darkens red beautifully without turning it muddy brown.

Essential Knowledge

Pro Mixing Tips

01

Use a White Palette

Always mix on a white surface. Any other color will distort how you perceive the mix — you'll think you have the right color until you apply it to the piece.

02

Test Small First

Mix a tiny test amount before committing to a large batch. Acrylic colors shift when they dry — always let your test swatch dry completely before judging the final color.

03

Write Down Your Recipes

If you're painting multiple pieces, note your proportions. Use approximate ratios like "3 parts + 1 part" so you can recreate it. You'll thank yourself when doing a second coat.

04

Don't Clean Between Similar Mixes

A slightly dirty brush between two adjacent colors creates beautiful natural gradients. This is a feature, not a bug — use it intentionally for blended transitions on your pieces.

05

Rescue Failed Colors

If a color feels off — too muddy, too bright, too cold — don't discard it. Too muddy? Add yellow. Too cold? Add a touch of red. Too dark? Add white + yellow. Every mix is fixable.

06

Add Water or Medium for Flow

A single drop of water or acrylic medium changes everything. It extends your working time, improves brush flow, and creates smoother coverage on textured clay surfaces.
